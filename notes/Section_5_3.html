    Subsection Problem 6

    First, notice the the modulus can be simplified:
    \begin{equation*} 1+2+\cdots + (p-1)=\frac{p(p-1)}{2}. \end{equation*}
    We need to show that
    \begin{equation*} \left. \frac{p(p-1)}{2} \right \rvert (p-1)!-(p-1). \end{equation*}
    Clearly
    \begin{equation*} \left. \frac{p-1}{2} \right \rvert p-1. \end{equation*}
    From Wilsonâ€™s Theorem we have
    \begin{align*} (p-1)! \amp \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \\ (p-2)! (p-1) \amp \equiv -1 \pmod{p} \\ (p-2)! \amp \equiv 1 \pmod{p}. \end{align*}
    That is,
    \begin{equation*} p | (p-2)!-1. \end{equation*}
    Since
    \begin{equation*} (p-1)!-(p-1)=(p-1)((p-2)!-1) \end{equation*}
    we have the desired result.
