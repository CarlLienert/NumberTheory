    Subsubsection Twin prime conjecture

    It is conjectured that there are infintitely many primes right next to each other. For example 3 and 5. Or 11 and 13. This is an open question: no one has given a proof one way or the other. This illustrates how difficult it is to prove things about primes.

    Subsubsection Goldbach conjecture

    This is yet another very easy to state but unproven conjecture: All integers \(n\) can be written
    \begin{equation*} n=p+q \end{equation*}
    where \(p,q\) are either prime or \(1\text{.}\) The discussion in the text illustrates how number theorists often do their work. They try to prove a weakened version of the result and work their way towards the stated conjecture. For example, all sufficiently large integers can be written as the sum of three odd primes has been proven.

    Subsection Problem 18

    Let \(p\) be a prime such that \(p \nmid b.\) The arithmetic progress is \(a+kb\) where \(k \in \mathbb{Z}.\text{.}\) Our goal is to produce the values of \(k\) such that \(p|a+kb.\) Since \(p \nmid b\text{,}\) there exist integers \(r\) and \(s\) such that
    \begin{equation} pr+bs=1.\tag{1} \end{equation}
    Let \(n_k=kp-as\text{.}\) This is the value weâ€™ll use for \(k\) in the arithmetic progression:
    \begin{align*} a+kb \amp = a+(kp-as)b\\ \amp = a-asb+kpb\\ \amp = a(1-sb)+kpb. \end{align*}
    Clearly \(p|kpb\text{.}\) Also \(p|1-sb\) since \(pr+bs=1\text{.}\) This gives the desired result: \(p|a+kb.\) Notice that each of these constructed terms are \(p\) apart:
    \begin{equation*} ((k+1)p-as)-(kp-as)=p. \end{equation*}
    If \(b\) is odd, then \(2 \nmid b\) so every other term is divisible by \(2.\)

    Subsection Problem 24

    Except for \(p=2\) and \(p=3\text{,}\) all primes are congruent to either \(1\) or \(5\) modulo 6. Suppose \(p\) is the first of a twin pair. That is, \(q=p+2\) is the second.

    If \(p\equiv 1 \pmod{6}\text{,}\) then \(q\equiv 3 \pmod{6}\) which is only prime if \(q=3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Thus, if \(p\) is the first of a twin prime pair, \(p\equiv 5 \pmod{6}\text{.}\) The second prime \(q\equiv 1 \pmod {6}.\) Then
    \begin{align*} pq-2 \amp \equiv 3 \pmod{6} \end{align*}
    which, again, is prime only if \(pq-2=3.\) But this would give \(pq=5\) which is impossible.
