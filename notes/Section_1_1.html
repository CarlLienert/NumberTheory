    Subsection Problem 6

    For \(n=4\text{:}\) \(4!=24\) and \(4^2=16\) so the inequality holds. Assume \(n!>n^2\) and consider:
    \begin{align*} (n+1)!\amp =(n+1)\cdot n!\\ \amp > (n+1)\cdot n^2 \text{ by the inductive hypothesis } \\ \amp > (n+1)\cdot (n+1)\\ \amp =(n+1)^2. \end{align*}
    The second inequality requires that we prove \(n^2>n+1\) for \(n\geq 4\text{.}\) The following inequalities are equivalent:
    \begin{align*} n^2 \amp > n+1\\ n^2-n-1 \amp >0\\ (n-2)(n+1)\amp >0. \end{align*}
    The last inequality is true if \(n<-1\) or \(n>2\text{.}\) So, \(n^2>n+1\) for \(n\geq 4 \text{.}\) We have shown that \(n!>n^2\) implies \((n+1)!>(n+1)^2\text{,}\) and we have shown the inequality is true for \(n=4\text{.}\) By induction, \(n!>n^2\) is true for all integers \(n\geq 4\text{.}\)

    Subsection Problem 8

    For \(n=1\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 2=\frac{2!}{1!}. \end{equation*}
    Assume the identity is true for \(n\) and consider:
    \begin{align*} 2\cdot 6 \cdot 10 \cdots (4n-2)\cdot (4n+2)\amp=\frac{(2n)!}{n!}\cdot (4n+2) \text{ by the inductive hypothesis}\\ \amp=\frac{(2n)!\cdot 2 \cdot (2n+1)}{n!}\\ \amp = \frac{(2n)!\cdot 2 \cdot (2n+1)\cdot (n+1)}{n!\cdot (n+1)}\\ \amp= \frac{(2n)! \cdot (2n+1)\cdot (2n+2)}{n!\cdot (n+1)}\\ \amp=\frac{(2(n+1))!}{(n+1)!}=\frac{(2n+2)!}{(n+1)!}. \end{align*}
    Thus, if the identity is true for \(n\text{,}\) then it is true for \(n+1\text{.}\) By induction the identity is true for all integers \(n\geq1\text{.}\) The inequality
    \begin{gather*} 2^n(n!)^2\leq(2n)! \end{gather*}
    is equivalent to
    \begin{gather*} 2^n(n!) \leq \frac{(2n)!}{n!} \end{gather*}
    so from part (a), we need to show that
    \begin{gather*} 2^n(n!) \leq 2\cdot 6 \cdot 10 \cdots (4n-2). \end{gather*}
    The right hand side is:
    \begin{align*} 2\cdot 6 \cdot 10 \cdots (4n-2)\amp=(2\cdot1)\cdot(2\cdot3)\cdot(2\cdot 5)\cdots (2\cdot (2n-1))\\ \amp=2^n\cdot (1\cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots (2n-1)) \end{align*}
    Clearly
    \begin{gather*} n! \leq (1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdot (2n-1)) \end{gather*}
    since there are \(n\) factors in both products and all but the first factor are greater in the right hand side. For example, if \(n=4\text{:}\)
    \begin{gather*} 1\cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 4 \leq 1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7. \end{gather*}
    Multiply both sides of
    \begin{gather*} n! \leq (1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdot (2n-1)) \end{gather*}
    by \(2^n\) to obtain the desired result.

    Subsection Problem 9

    For \(n=1\text{,}\) \(1+a \geq 1+a\text{.}\) Assume \((1+a)^n \geq 1+na\) and consider \((1+a)^{n+1}\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} (1+a)^{n+1}\amp=(1+a)^n(1+a)\\ \amp \geq (1+na)(1+a)\\ \amp =1+na+a+na^2\\ \amp =1+(n+1)a+na^2\\ \amp \geq 1+(n+1)a \end{align*}
    The first inequality follows from the inductive hypothesis. This first inequality is also where we need to use the assumption that \(1+a > 0\text{.}\) If \(1+a< 0\text{,}\) the direction of the inequality would change. The second inequality is true since \(na^2\geq 0\text{.}\)

    Subsection Problem 10

    (a) The following is not a complete proof or explanation. It is only the algebra part. Make sure you can identify where the induction hypothesis is used. Make sure you can justify both of the inequalities.
    \begin{align*} \frac{1}{1^2}+\frac{1}{2^2}+\dots+\frac{1}{n^2}+\frac{1}{(n+1)^2}\amp\leq 2-\frac{1}{n}+\frac{1}{(n+1)^2}\\ \amp =2-\left(\frac{1}{n}-\frac{1}{(n+1)^2}\right)\\ \amp=2-\left(\frac{(n+1)^2-n}{n(n+1)^2}\right)\\ \amp=2-\left(\frac{n^2+n+1}{n(n+1)^2}\right)\\ \amp=2-\left(\frac{n^2+n+1}{(n^2+n)(n+1)}\right)\\ \amp\leq 2-\left(\frac{n^2+n+1}{(n^2+n+1)(n+1)}\right)\\ \amp=2-\frac{1}{n+1} \end{align*}
    (b) Again, the following is only the algebra. It does not constitute a complete proof.
    \begin{align*} 2-\frac{n+2}{2^n}+\frac{n+1}{2^{n+1}}\amp=2-\left(\frac{n+2}{2^n}-\frac{n+1}{2^{n+1}}\right)\\ \amp=2-\frac{(2n+4)-(n+1)}{2^{n+1}}\\ \amp= 2-\frac{n+3}{2^{n+1}} \end{align*}
